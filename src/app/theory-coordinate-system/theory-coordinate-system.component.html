Theory: Coordinate systems

This is our first theory chapter in which I will explain the key difference between global and local coordinates and why they matter when working with Blender.

Take a look at the following image:

What you can see on the image above are several coordinate systems. There is one big central coordinate system with the y-axis facing the upwards direction. This from now on will be refered to as our global coordinate system. No matter what happens, the different axes will always stay in this exact same spot.

Now think of all those other smaller coordinate systems you see here as all the objects you add to your scene in Blender. All those objects are placed in Blender's global coordinate system, just at different positions. No matter how you scale or rotate an object, its position will always stay the same. In addition each of these objects also has a local coordinate system. As you can see in the image, all those small coordinate systems are somehow rotated versions of the big coordinate system. No matter how you rotate those, their position in the global coordinate system will always stay the same.
